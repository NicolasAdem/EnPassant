What's already out there
The chess tournament management space is actually pretty crowded — but most of it falls into two camps that both leave a real gap in the middle.

The "professional arbiter" camp — Swiss-Manager, Vega, WinTD, ChessManager, Tornelo. These are FIDE-compliant, do the math correctly, and tournament directors love them. They also look like Windows 98, have steep learning curves, cost money, and treat live spectator experience as an afterthought. Score entry is the director typing things into a desktop app while players queue up at their table.

The "modern but online-first" camp — Chess.com tournaments, Lichess Arena, Chess Nut, ChessHost. Sleek, mobile-friendly, easy to share. But Chess.com and Lichess assume games are played on their platform, not over a real board. Chess Nut and ChessHost are the closest competitors to what you're describing — they handle live standings for in-person events — but from what I read, they're still "director enters result" tools. Players don't confirm anything; there's no broadcast-quality projector view; no animation when someone climbs the leaderboard.

The specific gaps I see, that match what you described:

Player-side score confirmation. Almost nobody does this. The current norm is "director asks both players verbally, types it in." For scholastic events, national tournaments still use paper forms that both players sign physically. Doing this digitally — opponent gets a notification, taps confirm or dispute — is genuinely a fresh idea for in-person play.
Projector / "stadium screen" mode. This barely exists for amateur tournaments. Lichess and Chess.com have broadcast views, but only for their own online games. For a school running an over-the-board event, the current solution is "refresh the standings page on a laptop." A purpose-built TV/projector view with smooth animated rank changes when results come in — that's the showpiece.
Three-way pairing choice (Swiss / random / manual) in one tool. Most tools force you into Swiss. Some only do round-robin. The host being able to pick per-round is unusual.
Your real differentiator (in one line)
Everyone else builds for the director. You build for the room.

The animated, projector-friendly live leaderboard plus the dual-confirmation score entry are the unique combo. That's what makes it feel like a live sports broadcast instead of a spreadsheet.

Concept in plain words
Host opens the app, creates a tournament, adds players (with optional ELO), picks a pairing mode for each round (Swiss / random / manual), and projects the Live Board on the wall. After a game, one player taps their result on their phone, the opponent gets a ping and confirms or disputes, and the second the result locks, the leaderboard on the projector re-sorts with a smooth animation — names sliding up and down, the new leader briefly highlighted. The host has an override for disputes. Dark mode and green-accented throughout. Built with FastAPI on the backend (clean, modern, async-friendly, with WebSocket support for the live updates, which you'll absolutely need) and plain HTML/CSS/JS on the frontend.

A few things worth adding because they're cheap to build and make the app feel finished:

QR-code join for players (ChessHost does this and it's genuinely the right pattern — no accounts, no typing names).
Round timer on the projector view — adds drama, fills the screen between result updates.
"Just happened" ticker at the bottom of the projector view — "Alice beat Bob, climbing from #7 to #3" — this is the kind of small thing nobody else does.
Tiebreak handling — Swiss tournaments need Buchholz/Sonneborn-Berger to be taken seriously. We can implement the standard ones.
